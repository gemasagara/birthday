Happy Birthday!

on songs that carry you through

There are songs you listen to for pleasure, and then there are songs you listen to for survival. Happy End was the latter.

I used to put it on every morning — first thing, before anything else. Before the day could reach me. It was not a ritual born of joy but of necessity, the way you take a pill not because you want to, but because without it the hours ahead become unbearable. It was medication. It was armor. It was the only thing that made the threshold between sleep and waking feel like something I could cross.

"I still remember the cold. Not just the weather — the kind of cold that lives in your chest."

The mornings back then were sharp and gray. I'd walk through alleyways — the same ones every day — and there was something in the smallness of those paths, the particular quality of light between buildings in winter, that made everything feel both very heavy and very precise. Angst and despair have a way of sharpening the world even as they hollow you out. You notice everything. You feel everything. And it is exhausting in a way that doesn't translate well into words.

But the song was there. In my ears, in my chest. Something about the way it reaches — not toward resolution exactly, but toward the idea that things could resolve, that there is a version of ending that is also a kind of grace — made those alleyways survivable. It didn't fix anything. It just made the walking possible.

"Painful in terms of literally everything. But beautiful in the ways that I survived going through all of that."

I don't listen to it much anymore. Things are better now, genuinely — the kind of better that used to feel abstract and impossible. The mornings are different. I don't need the pill.

But occasionally the song finds me again. A shuffle, a memory, an algorithm that knows something I don't. And when it does, it's like a trapdoor opens — not downward, but sideways. Into those cold alleys. Into whoever I was then, standing in the half-dark with earbuds in, bracing. Surviving.

There's a particular kind of feeling that has no clean name: grief that is also gratitude. Missing a version of yourself even though that version was suffering. Feeling tender toward the hardest chapters precisely because you came out the other side of them. That is what Happy End brings back. Every time.

Some songs are just songs. And some songs are time capsules — sealed with pain, opened with memory, holding inside them an entire season of a life you nearly didn't make it through. This is one of those. I'm glad I had it. I'm glad I don't need it the way I once did. And I'm glad it's still there, waiting, for whenever I need to remember how far I've come.
